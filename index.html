About

Four lanes, one person.
The range is the point.

A polemical essay sharpens a strategy note; a fiscal federalism briefer grounds a political argument; a reading of the Shanti Parva clarifies a question about coalition management. The four lanes feed each other. That cross-pollination is the value.

I spent my early years running field networks in state elections: Karnataka, Odisha, Madhya Pradesh. Hiring field associates, managing political intelligence, learning what voters say when no one with a clipboard is listening. Then I moved into governance, drafting inputs for state legislation and building communications infrastructure. The distance between a policy announcement and a voter who understands it is where most governments lose. That distance is where I work.

What I do

Policy meets politics. Theory meets operation.

Political Framing of Policy

A reform is a document until it has a political frame: what it means for coalitions, for voter segments, for the narrative your opposition will try to set. You get a briefer that a decision-maker can act on, not one that sits in a folder.

Long-horizon Thinking

The next election is someone else's problem. I work on the structural risks and opportunities that surface in the cycle after: demographic shifts, institutional decay, coalition fault lines. You get early warning, not post-mortems.

Intellectual Architecture

Political theory, developmental economics, constitutional history: turned into strategy notes, positioning briefs, and published arguments. You get frameworks pressure-tested against Indian political reality, not imported templates.

Production Across Registers

A polemical essay and a fiscal federalism briefer require different voices. I produce both without mixing registers. You get the right output in the right format for the right audience: party leadership, bureaucrats, or the public.
